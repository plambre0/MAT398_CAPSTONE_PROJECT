Introduction:
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is a complex psychiatric disorder which occurs in those who
have experienced traumatic events and is characterized by a variety of debilitating symptoms including
intrusive memories or nightmares, avoidance behaviors, and negative changes in mood and cognition. Prior
research has concluded that the prevalence of PTSD on the south side of Chicago is significantly higher
than in the general population and suggests that there are conditions which may work to produce this
heightened prevalence.
While the south side of Chicago is known for its violence and systematic socioeconomic deprivation,
the significance to which violence may contribute to these phenomena is unclear. This analysis aims to
make inference of the relative significance and effects size of violence exposure on the prevalence of PTSD
controlling for relevant factors.

Results:
At a 95% confidence level, violent crime rate acted as a primary mediator for the relationship between
poverty and PTSD prevalence. Results from the Bayesian Structural Equation Model indicate that for
every one standard deviation increase in the normalized, cube-root-transformed violent crime rate, PTSD
prevalence is predicted to increase by 34% (IRR = 1.34, 95% CI [1.14,1.57]).
Poverty exerted a strong indirect effect on PTSD through its association with violent crime (Path A IRR = 2.08; Indirect IRR = 1.24). 
However, the direct effect of poverty on PTSD was negative (IRR = 0.77), resulting in a non-significant total effect (IRRtotal = 0.95). 
This suggests that in Chicago, the impact of poverty on prevalence of PTSD is primarily through the exposure to violent crime rather than
poverty in isolation.
